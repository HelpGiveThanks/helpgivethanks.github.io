A report like this one is typically thought to transfer information from one person to another. But this report is being written to document that such transfers of information are impossible.  Reports like this one do cause an illusion of information exchange.  It does appear to me, the writer, that at a minimum the text of this report seems to communicate the sounds of words.  No.  What both writer and reader are experiencing as this report, the words that seem to make up this report, are in fact hallucinations created by the brains of both writer and readers.  "You’ve never seen the real world, and you never will," says Steven Macknik at 4min 30sec into his and fellow brain researcher Susana Martinez-Conde's video presentation below.

 "...We’re not saying that the outside world isn’t out there. It is. But you’ve never lived there. Okay? You’ve never even been there for a visit [Reference: Macknik 2013]."  So, there is a report in the real world, but both writer and readers' brains will never see it.  This fact, that human brains have never had, and never will have access to the real world; that essentially hallucinating humans have been living and dealing with fellow hallucinating humans for all of our species existence, falsely believing that each person sees what everyone else sees, this fact has profound and amazing implications for our species and for artificial intelligence, like love is unconditional and cars can drive themselves, for example, which I will get to shortly.  But first, how are our brains creating what seems to be a shared experience of a single object: this report?  The late Walter Freeman, formerly of the The Freeman Laboratory for Nonlinear Neurodynamics in the Department of Molecular & Cell Biology at UC Berkeley, spent much of his career figuring this out, both to explain biological brains' ability to act into the real world while percieving an hallucination of it, and to duplicate this feat in artificially intelligent robots, which can now, all-by-themselves run factories, wharehouses, ports, drive cars, write essays, translate speech-into-text, etc.  Here is a brief summary of Freeman's action-perception model, which is "... simplified deliberately by lumping together many subsidiary components and lesser loops, in order to show the forest, not the trees [Freeman 1998, p8]."

Brains are all about control in the pursuit of self generated goals, "let's get something to eat," that start deep within brains at the entorhinal cortex (1). This amazing cortex helps create movie-like frames several times-a-second by combining inputs from all A) sensory systems (6): sight, sound, smell, feel, etc; and B) motor systems (2): position of our limbs, torso, neck, head, eyes, etc; with C) the location-and-timestamps of each sensory and motor input-event via the hippocampus (not shown).  For example, toe and nose impulses with the same timestamp will get to the brain at very different times, but be consciously experienced as happening simultainiously.  So, upon generating a goal (1), the brain sends out an action plan to the motor systems (2), and predictions to the sensory systems (6) of what the body's various sensory receptors — light, sound, etc. — will pickup along the way to achieving its goal.  

The sensory systems (6) now primed with predictions await the arrival of sensory-receptor (5) information, but first, the brain's motor plan (1) must be put into action!   And, so, the muscles move (3) in search of the predicted stimuli (4) at each milisecond-by-milisecond stage of the plan (1).  Thus, several-time-a-second, the muscles move (3) and the sensory receptors (5)  — red, green, blue (rgb) light sensitive cones in the eyes, for example — record the environmental impacts (4) on the moving (3) body.  The pattern of receptors (5) hit-and not-hit is faithfully passed on to the first level of brain tissue in all sensory systems (6), specifically those neurons connected directly to each sensory receptor (5) and primed with the brain's prediction (1).  NOTE: See references below for Freeman's diagrams and explanations of all sensory systems' (6) anatomy and physiology: Freeman 1997, Freeman 2001, and Freeman 2011.  All that gets passed on from the first to the second level neurons in each sensory system (6) is the statistical pass/fail information about the prediction: e.g., how many receptor predictions where correct?  The information about the pattern of receptors (5) hit-and-not-hit is washed away between the first and second level of neurons at this stage (6).  This discovery at first suprised Freeman, because it meant that brains don't use sensory receptors (5) to perceive the world, but to test (2 ➞ 6), as he would experimentally discover, the usefulness of the brain's own hallucinations (1).  In my remix of Freeman's Poetry of Brains talk below, he says, "What the animal knows [7] is only that which it has created [1] within itself.  It knows what expectations [1] it has, and it knows the results [6] of its tests [2 ➞ 6], or to put it another way, brains are continually engaged in creating hypotheses [1] and testing [2 ➞ 6] them.  And all they can know [7] is what did they hypothesize [1], and what was the result of the test [2 ➞ 6]... [Matson 2013]."
