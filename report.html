The common sense model of intelligence from the 17th century onward, does capture how we feel about what we appear to see, hear, feel, etc.  This feeling of a commonly sensed reality is false.  Please review the video evidence above to experience why it is false.  


Do compare the common sense and continuous education brain models below. Notice the common sense model shows us as basically enslaved to reality: "That pizza is making me hungry!" or "You make me _________."  We are not responsible according to common sense model for our feelings.  To control our feelings of disgust, desire, etc. we must control how others look, talk, behave, etc.  

We are victims of reality, according to the common sense model, fated to call it like we see it.  
The model on the right has multiple learn loops, each starting deep in the brain with a prediction of what our senses will detect as we move our bodies in pursuit of more and more control while we learn to achieve our goals.  In this model, notes the poet John Milton (1608–1674), "The mind is its own place, and in itself Can make a Heaven of Hell, a Hell of Heaven [Bloom 2011, 00:15:51]"


One of my great editors told me that my first description of Walter Freeman's continuous education model below was way too academic, so I hid it in the link below: Tour — Hallucination Testing (continuous education) Brain Diagram.  To take the basic tour of the digram just notice the multiple learn loops, and the need for our brains (according to this model) to test (1) hallucinatory predictions of what our (5) senses will detect, many times-per-second, as they move our (3) bodies to achieve our goals.

Walter Freeman noted that these learn loops are rather like frames in movie, "...a neural mechanism for the repetitive state transitions that initiate construction of cinematographic sequences, for which clinical evidence has been cited [Freeman 2007, p2]."  

In short, this model accounts for how brains come to be educated (grow tissue) to predict the meanings of things important to us like English, or Greek, or Spanish, etc.

In the references for this section, you'll find links to articles on fMRI brain-scan research showing distinct patterns of tissue growth in response to learning to golf, drive a London Taxi, etc.  


The continuous education model above was essential in guiding the invention of AIs that can now drive cars, identify cancers, etc.  Our biological brains, unlike an AI neural-network, can prime our sensory systems with many different types of hallucinatory-predictions based on our ever changing goals: drive a car, talk to a friend, look for cats, identify cancers, write an essay, do laundry, etc.  An AI neural-network is educated until it can predict one type of sensory information that fulfills a goal set by humans, like find and identitfy road signs.

Once an AI neural network (sensory system) is as good as or better than the very best person at predicting what we want, we humans end its education (the modification of its neural net). Then we copy it by the billions if we desire, or make it available over the internet to help anyone and everyone who needs it.  AIs stay on task 100% of the time and perform 24/7 as good or better than any human driving a car, or finding cancer cells, but not playing with a child, or enjoying a conversation with a friend, which requries task switching.  


The movie below does a great job of explaining how after millions of failures, AI neural networks gradually learn to get really good at predicting some small aspect of reality like identitify road signs.  No one knows how these AI neural networks do this!  The proof is in their predictive abilities that allows them drive cars, identify cancers, etc.

For example, a Tesla self-driving A.I. that learned on highways not streets, predicted a semi-truck's trailer was a road sign, and drove under it at high speed killing it's passenger. 

The Tesla A.I. was determined to not be at fault.  It was only supposed to be engaged when driving on highways not streets, which have cross-traffic.  Deciding what to learn, how well to learn, when to relearn, etc. are tasks that only self-organizing biological brains can do.

 2  Commons sense plus printing v. knowing truth by divine right

 "What the animal knows is only that which it has created within itself," notes Walter Freeman at 18min 24sec into my remix of his Poetry of Brains talk below. "It knows what expectations it has, and it knows the results of its tests, or to put it another way, brains are continually engaged in creating hypotheses and testing them. And all they can know is what did they hypothesize, and what was the result of the test... [Matson 2013]." "We cannot know the world by direct experience of it. [Freeman 1996]."  Freeman could not have made these research lab discoveries if he had lived 400 years earlier.  In the 17th century, the belief in common sense and the superiority of printed facts to human memories was just beginning to transition Europe away from the power of monarchs and church officials to imprison, torture, execute, etc. anyone challenging their divine right to know the truth like, for example, Galileo Galilei (1564–1642).  The common sense model dramatically increased the percentage of white males accepted as having useful minds, but as with the divine-right model, the common-sense model was deeply flawed, and not based on any brain research, just common sense: "I think. Therefore, I am" —Descartes (1596–1650).    
In addition to the problem that the common-sense brain-model is a just-so story for explaining the sexist, racists, etc. status quo of 17th century Europe, this model also has several problems of a purely mechanical, "how does it do that?" nature: How does the reasoning mind (consciousness) interface with the biological brain at step four in the diagram below to both A) receive sensory information (3 ➞4a) and then tell the motor systems how to respond to it (4a ➞5)?  How does the brain know what sensory information means: bind stored knowledge (stored memories) to sensory information?  If all people have brains that, "Call it like they see it," then how come some people know better than others?  This last problem is the root of the false belief that brains develop instead of grow; that the biology of gender, race, etc. determines the level of intelligence/reason our brains can develop.  (Click the links to Paul Cisek's "Beyond the computer metaphor" article and related videos provided in the references below for a wonderful history and analysis of the common sense model.) 

The common sense model has a lot of problems, but combined with the printing press, its 17th century adoption was a significant philosophical advancement that Freeman gives credit to for the rise of the middle class and, "the belief in the capability of [white male] individuals to accept responsibility and take action to change things in the world that needed changing ... [and also for] the growth of science and technology through the later Middle Ages [Freeman 1998, p3]." Historian James Burke describes the importance of the marriage of common sense philosophy and printing in the video below: "[38min 30sec] Once the ancient traditional authority in something-or-other was down in print, then, for the first time, a whole mass of people could go out and check up on them, which of course a whole mass of people promptly did! ... [For example, a] pharmacist would take his new printed copy on some classic on plants and see how it compared with the real thing. From 1550 on the science of direct observation flowered ... [Burke 1985].”

Common sense replaced the religious hierarchy of knowing truth by divine right, and the printed page replaced human memory for storing facts.  Notes Burke, "[40min 55sec] You didn't need to remember. If you read the latest most definitive book on something, well you where up to speed with the best in the field. ... [42min 30sec] And now people no longer had to go through years and years of apprenticeship to learn something thanks to an avalanche of do-it-yourself manuals that taught them industrial skills. ... [45min 6sec] Where once you needed the physical presence of people who knew things, now you could hold their minds in your hand [Burke 1985]."

 Tour — Hallucination Testing (continuous education) Brain Diagram

The action-perception cycle starts anatomcially with the entorhinal cortex/hallucination start (1), which combines inputs from all A) sensory systems (6): sight, sound, smell, feel, etc; and B) motor systems (2): position of our limbs, torso, neck, head, eyes, etc; with C) the location-and-timestamps of each sensory and motor input-event via the hippocampus (not shown). These combined inputs form something like a frame in a movie. The entorhinal cortex (1) creates each frame by sending out action plans to the motor systems (2), and predictions to the sensory systems (6) of patterns that will form on different sensory receptors (5) as a result of each action. Frame-by-frame, the muscles move (3) and the sensory receptors (5) — red, green, blue (rgb) light sensitive cones in the eyes, for example — record patterns of stimuli (4) impacts that are then processed by the sensory systems (6).

These patterns of stimuli receptors hit-and-not-hit (5) are faithfully passed on as expected to the first level of neurons in all sensory systems (6) — neurons that where primed with pattern predictions (1) at the start of the action-perception cycle. And then the unexpected happens! Only the pass/fail information about each prediction is passed on to the next level of neurons in all sensory systems (6)! The information about the pattern of receptors hit-and-not-hit (5) is completey washed away between the first and second level of neurons in all systems(6). NOTE: See references below for links to Freeman's diagrams and explanations of sensory systems' (6) washing-away-information anatomy and physiology: Freeman 1997, Freeman 2001, and Freeman 2011. This discovery at first suprised Freeman, because it meant that brains cannot be using sensory receptors (5) to perceive the world. Instead, as he would experimentally discover, our brains use them (5) to test the usefulness of its own sensory-receptor predictions (1) — predictions (1) that we consciously experience (7) as, "the real world."
